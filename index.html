Three-minute Training

【30】Japan has a serious problem with English. On the TOEFL iBT test of Reading, Listening, Speaking, and Writing, Japan ranks 29th out of 31 in Asia. In the 2017 EF English Proficiency Index, Japan is 37th out of 80 non‑English speaking countries and is in the “Low” group, below South Korea, China, and Vietnam. One main cause is the old Grammar Translation Method from the Meiji era. In this way, students read, learn rules, and translate English into Japanese, often with backward translation. The goal is correct Japanese, not real English use. So many people have school knowledge but cannot communicate. 　Time is also a big issue. Students learn English for six years in middle and high school, with about 790 hours in class. This is far less than 2,200 hours, the time that English native speakers may need for Japanese for work use. The total text in six years is only 72–73 pages of a normal English book. This is too little, so progress is slow. 　Sound training is almost ignored. English and Japanese have very different sounds, stress, and rhythm. With poor work on speaking and listening, students cannot speak clearly or understand well. Old style entrance exams keep this problem and block change.

Quiz

Japan’s English education is effective, and students gain strong speaking skills through many hours of pronunciation training. Japan’s low scores on TOEFL iBT and the 2017 EF EPI show serious problems in English education, including translation-focused teaching, too little study time, and weak work on pronunciation. The main causes of Japan’s low English ability are the old Grammar Translation Method, only about 790 class hours over six years (far less than 2,200 hours), very small reading input (about 72–73 pages), and little focus on speaking and listening. Japan ranks at the top in Asia for English and proves that grammar translation and entrance exams are the best way to build communication skills.
